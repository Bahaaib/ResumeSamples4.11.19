Weight Loss Eating Plan

The importance of Carbohydrates

I'm sure you've all heard someone at one point tell you that when it comes to weight loss 'Carbs are the enemy'. This is just not true.

Carbohydrates are the major source of energy and nutrients in our diet. They provide the body with an immediate energy store.

If our body does not take in enough Carbohydrates, then our blood sugar levels will drop and we will find ourselves lacking energy.

One of the main types of Carbohydrates in our body is a sugar called Glucose. Glucose is vital. Many organs need Glucose in order to function, the brain being probably the most important one.

Proteins and fats can also be broken down in the body to produce glucose. However the process using protein to produce glucose breaks down body tissue and can be potentially harmful.

Ketosis is the process whereby your body converts fat into energy. Ketosis will occur during Low Carbohydrate diets. Ketones are a by-product during this conversion and it is these Ketones that can give you the bad breath often associated with Low Carbohydrate diets.

By depriving our bodies of Carbohydrates and therefore glucose, our bodies depend on fat as the energy source. This will increase the ketone levels in your blood. The increased acidity in your blood as a result of these ketones, can be dangerous as it may eventually lead to serious damage to your liver and kidneys

So despite what you may have read elsewhere, high protein diets are not really beneficial to your body.

Carbohydrates are stored on our body as glycogen. This is what we use when we need an immediate energy fuel.

Carbohydrates are therefore very important when it comes to exercising. If we consume a sufficient amount of complex carbohydrates in our diet (i.e. rice, pasta, potatoes) the glycogen stored in our muscles and liver will be enough to ensure a peak performance. Someone who is on a low carbohydrate diet will not be able to perform at a high level as their bodies will tire very quickly.

Their glycogen levels need to be restored after an exercise session, as they will have just used up a lot of glycogen stores. It is advised to consume a meal high in carbohydrates within half an hour of exercise to re-fuel.

Carbohydrates should provide about 50 % of the energy in your diet. Non-milk extrinsic sugars i.e. the sugar added in manufactured foods,

should make up no more than 10% of the energy

Proteins

Proteins are essentially the building blocks in our body. They carry out a variety of functions:

Structural role - found in skin, hair and connective tissue

Protective role - helps fight infection and is involved in the blood clotting process

Storage role - Stores oxygen in muscles

Transport role - Transports oxygen through the bloodstream

Contractile role - Assists in muscular contractions and helps to improve strength and power.

Protein can be found from both animal and plant proteins as shown in the lists below:

Animal protein:

Chicken

Red meat

Fish

Dairy products

Eggs

As these are high in amino acids, animal proteins are referred to as complete. They are also high in saturated fat (which we will look at later in the book) and low in fibre so should be eaten in the right proportions.

Plant protein:

Nuts

Fruit and vegetables

Seeds

Beans

Plant proteins should generally be consumed in higher amounts than animal proteins.

In order to keep the correct dietary balance, try to select animal proteins that are low in saturated fat i.e. fish and poultry.

Some people believe that vegetarians do not consume enough protein. This is not true as we can get protein from a number of non-animal foods.

A good example of high protein meals for vegetarians could include:

Beans on toast

Lentil curry

Tortillas and mixed beans

Hoummus

Fats

Fats or lipids carry out a number of roles within the body. Although it is important that we don't consume too much fat we do need a certain amount as it provides a variety of important functions.

One of the most notable uses for fat is as an energy source and this energy can be stored in the body. It also insulates the body, as it is a slow conductor of heat.

Our GDA (guideline daily amount) for fat is 70 g for women and 95g for men.

The two main types of fat you will come across are saturated and unsaturated.

Saturated

The saturated fats are those that are solid at room temperature. Examples of these are cheese and butter.

If you were to think of fats as blocks, saturated fats would be rectangular shaped almost like a brick. This shape enables saturated fats to build up quite easily.

Therefore, this type of food should not be consumed in high quantities as the build up of the saturated fats can raise cholesterol levels and increase the risk of heart disease.

Our GDA for saturated fat is 20g for women and 30g for men

Unsaturated

There are two types of unsaturated fat. These are mono-unsaturated and poly-unsaturated. These can be beneficial in helping to reduce blood cholesterol.

Again using the block analogy, unsaturated fats are not flat rectangular shapes and as a result, it is more difficult to clog up your arteries by consuming these fats. When choosing foods it is advisable to select those that are high in unsaturated fat. These types can be found in olive oil and sunflower oil.

It is also important to eat oily fish such as salmon, mackerel or herring. These contain an essential fatty acid called omega 3 polyunsaturates. This type of fatty acid is particularly good for protecting the heart against the risk or coronary heart disease.

Further types of fats, that may potentially increase the risk of coronary heart disease, are trans fats. These can form as a result of liquid oil being turned into solid fat from a process called hydrogenation.

These fats may be listed on food ingredients as hydrogenated vegetable oil. They are also found in some biscuits, cakes, pastry and other processed foods.

To stay healthy it is important to eat a certain amount of fat. However it is very important to ensure you eat the right kind of fat. Eating too much saturated fat and trans fat could be detrimental to your health. Therefore it is best to opt for food that is high in unsaturated fat instead.

When it comes to weight management it is particularly important to control the amount of fat consumed in your diet. Any excess fat not used as energy will be stored and inevitably lead to weight gain.

Sugars

Sugar is found naturally in fruit and vegetables. It is advisable to reduce your intake of foods and drinks that contain added sugar. These foods include cakes, biscuits, and chocolate. Fizzy drinks should also be avoided.

Blood sugar levels will affect your decision when choosing what to eat. We will talk about this more in the coming sections. In short, the foods containing added sugar only give us a "quick fix". When our energy levels dip again, we crave the sugary snacks to boost our energy levels. It then becomes a vicious cycle where we snack on a poor choice of foods.

The solution to this would be to snack on fruit. This will satisfy your bodies' sugar requirements and will avoid the slump in energy after the "quick fix".

Fizzy drinks should be avoided where possible as they are gassy and therefore can be filling and may stop us from eating healthier foods.

Also try to reduce the amount of sugar added to hot drinks, i.e. coffee and tea. Although you may think you need it for the taste, this is something you can cut out gradually until you no longer need to add any sugar to your drink.

When checking on food ingredients lists you can see how much sugar is included. This may be listed as Carbohydrates (of which sugars). The biggest ingredients will be listed first. Therefore if your food contains a high amount of sugar it will be listed near the top of an ingredients list or food label.

It is worth noting that sugar on food labels can also be described as fructose, glucose or sucrose.

Salt

The recommended daily requirement for salt is 4g (1.6g sodium). The maximum acceptable level is 6g (2.3g sodium). This recommended daily requirement will be met by our daily diet. Therefore, there is no need to add any salt to food.

Salt will be listed as sodium on food labels.

If sodium is consumed in large quantities it can lead to an increase in blood pressure. This in turn can increase the risk of stroke or coronary heart disease.

You may be surprised by the amount of salt in some food such as soups, bread and cereal. It is therefore advisable to always check food labels to make comparisons.

There is also a high salt content in ready meals. This content will be listed on the label as sodium per 100g.

1.25g or 0.5g of sodium per 100g would be considered a high salt content and 0.25g or 0.1g of sodium per 100g would be considered a low salt content.

Always taste your food before adding salt to it. To avoid the temptation of adding salt to your meals, don't put salt on the dinner table. Instead try adding herbs or spices to make food more appealing to your palate. Lemon juice is also a very effective way of adding flavour to your meal.

Food labelling

Food labelling is something you should be aware of. You may be mislead by certain marketing on packaging that leads you to believe that you are purchasing a food that is low in fat. For example, foods that are described as "reduced" or "lower" means that it is not necessarily a low fat product, it just has less fat than the original.

For a product to be "low-fat" it must have less than 3g of fat per 100g.

It is also worth noting a few other terms when it comes to food labelling.

Organic - This means that the particular food must consist of at least 95% of organic ingredient.

Farm Fresh - a meaningless term with no legal definition

No added sugar - means that no sugar is included in the product, however sweetener may have been added

Fat free - Means that the product should have less than 0.15g of fat per 100g of the product.

Reduced salt - Means that the product should contain less than 0.5g of sodium per 100g.

Food labelling can be confusing, so we recommend that you pay close attention to the ingredient list. In the instance that sugar and fat are listed first on the ingredients list, you will know that this is a product high in sugar or fat.

Traffic Light Labelling

A growing number of supermarkets are now using traffic light food labelling. This labelling system is designed to make it easier to choose healthier options for eating.

Food labels will be categorised with content of fat, saturated fat, salt and sugar.

With traffic light colours, you can see at a glance if the food you're looking at has high, medium or low amounts of each of these nutrients per 100g of the food. The colours are as follows:

Red = High

Amber = Medium

Green = Low

So following this, a red light on the front of the pack would indicate that the food is high in something we are trying to cut down on. We should try and keep consumption of these foods to a minimum or at least consume in moderation.

Amber indicates that a food isn't particularly high or low in the nutrient so it would be a suitable choice most of the time as part of a healthy balanced diet. It would also be advised to go for green in that nutrient at least some of the time.

Green means a particular food is low in that nutrient and therefore the more green lights, the healthier the choice.

Most foods in the shops will have a mixture of Red, Amber and Green. Overall we are looking to go for more foods labelled with Greens and Ambers, and fewer Reds in order for us to make the healthier choice.

The key is all about finding the right balance of our diet.

Protein Shakes For Weight Loss

Nothing can be quite as delicious and satisfying as a great diet shake, especially in the hot weather months that we are now enjoying.

Whether you choose to go to the mall and visit a nutritional supplement shop, so you can add supplements to your diet shake recipes or whether you prefer your diet shake recipes to contain just what you can get at the farm stand or grocery store; there are a million diet shake recipes out there. It can also be quite fun to experiment with your diet shake recipes and add your own personal touches to your delicious creations.

One of my favorite ingredients to add to any smoothie or diet shake recipes is low-fat yogurt. No mater whether you are making a water-based (ice) shake or a dairy-based (2 % milk) diet shake recipe, a little container of yogurt can add a lot.

Yogurt adds a nice creamy consistency to your diet shake recipes, and I wholeheartedly recommend that you try it in your favorite smoothie recipe the first chance you get.
