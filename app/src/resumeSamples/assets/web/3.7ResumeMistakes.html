7 Resume Mistakes Sure to Put You in the Reject Pile
by Chris Johannes

The average time an initial resume screener (recruiter, HR rep, hiring manager or someone else) looks at your resume the first time is less than 15 seconds. It's true. Look it up. Some say it's as little as 10 seconds. Mind you, this is only the amount of time they spend looking at it to decide if they want to look at it further and in more detail. In other words, you have, on average, 10 - 15 seconds to get the attention of the screener.

Some things you can't control. If you applied to a position you clearly aren't qualified for (like a Controller position when your experience is all Wed Development) then there is nothing you can do. You just wasted your time applying and 10 - 15 seconds of the screener's life. Don't do this. It's just irritating. But there are things you can avoid when writing your resume that will help keep you in the "read further" pile.

Many of the mistakes candidates make are based on outdated thinking. Other mistakes are just poor judgment or a lack of understanding of how to put together a resume. If you're applying to jobs that you think you're qualified for but getting no responses then it's probably time to revisit your resume and perhaps look to hire a professional resume writing service. But let's see if you can avoid that with these tips.

1) Your Objective
Often people include an objective on their resume. Sometimes it's a very bland, generic objective that either says you have no ambition or is a waste of space. Other times it's specific, but not relevant to the job you're applying for. You say something like "Seeking a position where I can utilize my extensive management experience‚EUR¶". But the position doesn't supervise anyone. Even though you're okay with this you've already knocked yourself out.

Solution: Skip the Objective. There is no real reason to include one. It adds no value to your resume and can only hurt you. Instead, lead off with a "Summary" of qualifications and experience. Make a bullet list of your skills and accomplishments. If you really must include an Objective out of some deep seeded need then make sure you cater it to each job you apply to.

2) Poor Layout / Organization
You want to keep your resume to one or two pages and cram everything in. You make it one, long winded paragraph without any breaks. It's so crammed it's basically unreadable. If a screener can't easily see your relevant experience then they aren't going to waste their time reading your resume. Don't make them dig for the information. Lay your resume out in a professional manner. Make sure your previous employers and positions are clearly identified with space breaks between the end of one job and the start of the next. Also, use bullet points as much as possible to identify your skills and experience. They are easier to read than a long winded paragraph.

Also, if you are a recent graduate with little experience then your Education is key and should be listed at the start of your resume (after the summary). If you have a few years' experience then move the Education to the bottom of your resume, after your experience.

3) Including Too Much Information
If you have previous experience, great. But you don't need to go all the way back to the stone ages. Make sure you include at least the last 10 years and up to 15. If you want to include experience further than that keep it short. List the companies you worked, the position you held and time frame. Other than that is it really relevant to the current position your applying to? A resume can be longer than one page (and should be if you have a few years' experience and more than one job). But you don't want to kill more trees than necessary. Try and keep the resume to 3 pages. If you're a technical person with lots of listed skills or publications these should be listed at the end of the resume and not counted against the "3 page" recommendation.

Also, if you are now a professional with at least 2 or 3 years' experience then lose the jobs you held while in school. No one cares if you were a sales clerk at Macy's if your applying as a Senior Accountant.

4) Skipping Jobs you've held with one Company (Promotions)
You list only you're last position held with a previous or current employer even though you were with them for several years and earned promotions. This will cause the screener to think you didn't progress at all in your job. Why would that be? If you held several positions then include them separately. Indicate you were promoted to the next position you held. You don't need to list the company multiple times, but make it clear the positions were all with the same employer. I've seen many resumes discounted before because it looked like the candidate was a major job hopper when in fact they were stable and progressing.

5) Resume Format
The functional format is a format some candidate use for their resume. Don't. While it does list all your skills and accreditations in a nice and clear format, it can be confusing and doesn't tie your experience with when and where you got it. Use a reverse chronological resume format. Start with your most recent employer and work backwards. Include the skills you learned/used at each position. This format is much easier to read and identify where you obtained your experience which will get the attention of the screener. Remember, you need them to want to continue reading your resume.

6) Keywords
You right your resume in a very simple, easy flowing way. You try and keep it short and write it without regard to the critical components of the job you're applying too. This could be fine but is becoming more and more dangerous. More and more companies are using automated programs to screen through the resumes they receive to identify potential candidates for human review. The key to getting past this and having your resume seen by a human is Keywords.

Review the job description for the job you're applying to carefully and make sure you include those keywords they are looking to identify. And make sure you put them in similar order, spelling and case that the description has. In other words, if the company requires "jQuery" then list it that way on your resume. If they are looking for "jquery" then use the lower case "q". Be careful, however. You don't want to clutter your resume with too many repeated keywords.

7) Lack of Professionalism
Your email address is fatmama@emaildomain.com or ilikeithot@emaildomain.com. Great for you. Your friends all know it. But you don't want those to identify you to a prospective employer. Get a simple, more professional email address. You can use Gmail, Yahoo or any number of other free email service providers. Just make it professional (use your name if possible; add a number to it if your name isn't available as is).

Also, make sure your voicemail message is professional. Keep it simple in case a prospective employer calls and leaves you a message.
